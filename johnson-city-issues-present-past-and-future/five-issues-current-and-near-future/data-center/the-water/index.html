The water footprint of a data center introduces a hidden, heavy-duty physical reality that completely contradicts the sleek, "cloud" marketing. It is a brilliant second chapter for your pastor's packet.

You can break the water crisis down into three distinct, eye-opening layers that directly support your argument for community stewardship over corporate haste.

  1. The On-Site "Evaporative Cooling" Strain

Computers generate intense heat. To prevent millions of processors from melting down, the most energy-efficient method for a data center is an evaporative cooling system.

Essentially, these warehouses operate like massive, hyper-industrial swamp coolers. They pull in millions of gallons of fresh water, run it over the hot equipment, and let it evaporate into the sky to carry the heat away.

The Scale: A large data center can consume up to 5 million gallons of water a day—the same amount of water used by a small town of up to 50,000 people.

The Peak Demand Trap: Tech companies often provide "annual averages" to local boards to make their usage look modest. But university research shows that on blazing hot summer days, their water demand can spike six to ten times higher than their average. This means that on the hottest days of the year—the exact days when local families are watering gardens, filling pools, and straining the municipal water supply—the data center is aggressively sucking down the community's water reserves just to cool its chips.

  1. The "Double Whammy" of Indirect Water Use

This is the hidden layer that most city planners completely miss, and it will be invaluable for your packet.

A data center hits the water ecosystem twice. Even if a data center uses a "dry" cooling system on-site to placate local zoning boards, it still requires massive amounts of electricity. Generating that electricity at a standard regional power plant (like a TVA thermal or hydro facility) requires millions of gallons of freshwater withdrawals for steam and cooling turbines.

Experts refer to this as the indirect water footprint, and it is often two to four times larger than the water evaporated on-site. When a tech company boasts about a "zero-water" facility, they are often just pushing the massive water drain down the road to the regional utility's water tables and rivers.

  1. The Theological Pivot: Controlling the Rains vs. Counting the Gallons

This ties beautifully back into your conversation about Job and Genesis. In the ancient world, water was recognized as the ultimate sign of God's direct sovereignty and provision.

In Job 38:25-27, God explicitly humbles humanity by asking:

"Who cuts a channel for the torrents of rain, and a path for the thunderstorm, to water a land where no one lives, an uninhabited desert, to satisfy a desolate wasteland and make it sprout with grass?"

Water is a sacred, gift meant to sustain life, grow food, and keep a community healthy.

When a city rushes into an industrial tech agreement out of FOMO, they are allowing a corporate ledger to commodify a local watershed. They are prioritizing the processing of digital data over the physical lifeblood of the region.

By demanding due diligence and refusing to rush, your community is boldly declaring: Our water belongs to the landscape, the farmers, and the families God placed here. We will not drain the natural blessings of our local springs and rivers just to keep an artificial tech trend from overheating.
