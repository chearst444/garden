The backstory on the Limestone facility explains exactly why BrightRidge is acting with such urgency right now, and how they ended up holding that parcel on Innovation Drive.

When and Why the Limestone Facility Happened

The Limestone facility—an industrial-scale cryptocurrency mining operation run by Red Dog Technologies—opened in 2021. It was built on land owned by BrightRidge directly adjacent to an existing electrical substation.

At the time, BrightRidge promoted it as a massive win for the utility: it was a "high-usage customer" that swallowed up surplus power during off-peak hours. Red Dog became BrightRidge's single largest industrial client, drawing a staggering 12 megawatts of power—roughly 7% of BrightRidge's entire electricity sales, or the equivalent of powering nearly 9,800 homes. The revenue from this single site heavily subsidized the utility, keeping residential electric rates low for everyone else in the area.

The Fallout: "A Jet Plane Idling"

The problems started almost immediately after the switches were flipped. ==To keep thousands of high-speed computer servers from overheating, the facility relied on a massive array of heavy-duty industrial cooling fans running 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

Because the facility was built just 100 yards away from nearby homes in Washington County's quiet New Salem community, the noise pollution was devastating. Local residents described the continuous, low-frequency roar as sounding like "a jet plane idling" in their backyards. The relentless hum led to severe sleep deprivation, immense community outrage, and organized public protests.

The public backlash was so severe that Washington County formally sued #Red/Dog Technologies and its landlord, BrightRidge, citing severe zoning violations and public nuisance.

The Settlement and the Financial Clock

To avoid a protracted legal disaster, a multi-party settlement was reached in November 2023. Under the binding terms of that court agreement:

  1. Red Dog Technologies was permitted to continue operating the Limestone data center for a strictly limited grace period, mandating a final shutdown and full equipment removal by no later than March 2026.

  2. As a penalty and community concession, Red Dog and BrightRidge were forced to split a $150,000 bill to provide free high-speed fiber internet to all the heavily impacted neighbors in the Limestone area while the site finished out its operations.

Why BrightRidge Bought the Innovation Drive Parcel

The hard deadline of March 2026 put BrightRidge on a clock. Losing 7% of their total power revenue when Limestone went dark meant they were facing an immediate financial crater. To offset this loss and avoid forcing a major rate hike onto everyday local residential customers, BrightRidge's board needed to find a replacement data tenant immediately.

Knowing they had to completely evacuate the rural Limestone neighborhood, BrightRidge set its sights on the Buffalo Mountain Industrial Park inside Johnson City limits, purchasing the vacant parcel on Innovation Drive. Their strategy was simple: buy land zoned for heavy industry (I-2), build a brand-new facility, and relocate high-density computing infrastructure there, hoping that being inside an official industrial park would shield them from the kind of residential zoning lawsuits they lost in Limestone.

To put it bluntly: they are trying to fix a problem they created in the county by moving it right into the city limits. The county neighbors organized, fought back, and successfully used the legal system to declare the facility a zoning violation and a public nuisance, forcing a court-ordered eviction by March 2026.

Instead of taking that as a sign to completely rethink the business model, the utility essentially said, "Okay, we can't operate in the unincorporated county anymore, so let's buy land in an official city industrial park on Innovation Drive and try again". They assumed that the heavy industrial (I-2) zoning tag would give them a legal shield against lawsuits.

What they didn't count on was the city residents paying attention. The neighborhood advocates in south Johnson City looked at the exact same play and said, "Absolutely not—you aren't dumping the county's noise problem into our backyard".
