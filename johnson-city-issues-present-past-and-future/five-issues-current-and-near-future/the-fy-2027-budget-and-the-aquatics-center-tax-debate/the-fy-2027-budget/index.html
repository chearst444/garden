The passage of the Fiscal Year 2026–2027 (FY2027) budget by the Johnson City Board of Commissioners on June 25, 2026, marked the culmination of intense public debate. The final vote was 4–1, with Mayor Greg Cox, Vice Mayor Jenny Brock, and Commissioners Todd Fowler and Joe Wise voting in favor, while Commissioner Whitney Goetz cast the dissenting vote.  

The primary catalyst for controversy was a substantial property tax increase linked closely to the funding of a massive capital project: a new, comprehensive municipal aquatics center.  

  1. The Property Tax Hike & Budget Impact

The approved #FY2027 budget institutes a ==28-cent property tax increase, raising the city’s property tax rate from $1.38 to $1.66 per $100 of assessed value. ==

 The Math for Homeowners: This represents ==an approximate 20% increase in the municipal property tax rate.For a home with an assessed market value of $300,000, the change amounts to an additional $210 per year in city property taxes. ==
 

 Purpose: While the overall budget covers general city operations, public safety, and infrastructure, the 28-cent hike was specifically pursued to absorb the long-term debt service required to fund major capital projects, spearheaded by the Aquatics Center.  

A $37 million aquatics center, projected to open on the campus of the Memorial Park Community Center

While the aquatics center took up almost all the oxygen in the room during the budget hearings, the final $130 million General Fund budget actually includes several other major funding shifts for the city.
If you look past the pool debate, the rest of the 28-cent tax hike is being broken down into a few specific buckets for city services and community projects:

  • Boosting Core Infrastructure (Paving): The city allocated an extra $500,000 specifically for road paving and neighborhood street repairs. During public comment, many residents complained that neighborhood streets were crumbling while the city was looking at luxury projects, so this cash injection was added to directly address those complaints.
  • Expanding City Field Staff: Part of the new revenue is earmarked to fund entirely new positions and core-service crews. The goal is to scale up Public Works and maintenance teams to keep up with the rapid growth and high-density development popping up all over the city.
  • Replenishing the Stabilization Fund: A portion of the tax increase is going right back into the city's rainy-day stabilization fund. The city had to draw down on its reserves recently due to rising operational costs and inflation, so this budget is designed to rebuild that safety net to protect Johnson City's strong municipal credit rating.
  • BrightRidge Held Rates Flat: On a positive financial note for residents, right around the exact same time the city passed this budget, BrightRidge (the local public power and broadband provider) officially adopted its own $287 million budget for the coming year with zero local rate increases. They are holding both electric and internet subscriber rates completely flat while putting $8 million into expanding their high-speed fiber network into surrounding rural pockets like Sulfur Springs and Gray.

Essentially, the city's leadership framed the entire FY2027 package as a necessary correction to keep services running smoothly, add boots on the ground for infrastructure, and catch up with a rapidly expanding population.
