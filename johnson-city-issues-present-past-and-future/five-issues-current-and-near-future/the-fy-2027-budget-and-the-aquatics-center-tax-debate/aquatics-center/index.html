The $37 Million #AquaticsCenter

The headline feature of the budget is the full funding allocation for a $37 million aquatics center, projected to open on the campus of the Memorial Park Community Center (510 Bert Street). When accounting for the interest on a 20-year bond issue to fund construction, the total cost to taxpayers is estimated to reach roughly $55 million.  

According to the city’s Aquatic Center Feasibility Study, the facility is designed as a phased build-out to include:  

 An indoor competition pool capable of hosting regional swim meets and school training.  

 An indoor leisure pool featuring play structures and warm-water therapeutic spaces.  

 Outdoor water amenities and a community splash pad.  

  1. Core Points of Tension & Debate

The budget workshops and consecutive readings exposed a stark philosophical divide between city leadership and vocal segments of the community:

 The "Quality of Life" Stance: Mayor Cox and the majority of the commission defended the budget as a vital, long-term investment in community wellness and economic development. They pointed out that Johnson City’s existing public pool infrastructure is aging out and that a modern facility would generate economic revenue via swim meets while serving families and local schools. Even with the 28-cent hike, leadership emphasized that Johnson City's tax rate remains competitive compared to several surrounding municipalities.  

 The Fiscal Responsibility Pushback: Citizens and organizations opposing the budget argued that a luxury recreational facility is the wrong priority during a time of broader economic anxiety, especially for seniors and residents on fixed incomes. Opponents argued that the city should prioritize core infrastructure, road maintenance, and public safety before taking on massive new debt. Commissioner Goetz introduced an eleventh-hour amendment during the final reading to remove the aquatics center funding from the budget entirely, but the amendment failed 4–1.  

 The Freedom Hall Pool Crisis: The closure of the Freedom Hall Pool on April 3 escalated the debate. Local swim clubs, high school teams, and parents noted that combining programs into remaining spaces stripped local youth of 36% to 87% of their water time. Because the new $37 million center won't open until approximately 2028, a major point of community friction was the lack of an immediate "bridge plan". ==Critics noted that while the new facility costs tens of millions, the existing Freedom Hall Pool could be repaired for an estimated $771,000 to $848,000. In response, the commission ultimately directed city staff to fast-track bids to repair Freedom Hall alongside the long-term aquatics center plan. ==

However, Legion street Pool's future is directly tied to this new project. According to the city's official Aquatic Center Feasibility Study, both the closed Freedom Hall Pool and the outdoor Legion Street Pool have officially reached the "end of their service life". They suffer from compromised older plumbing systems and high maintenance costs.  

The city's long-term plan is to centralize its primary public swim programming. The new $37 million mega-facility at Memorial Park is designed to act as the modern, all-in-one replacement for what Johnson City is losing as these older pools age out.  

What happens to Legion Street Pool right now?

For the immediate future, Legion Street Pool is operating as usual for its normal seasonal schedule (Memorial Day to Labor Day). Because the city is facing a major shortage of public pool space while Freedom Hall is shut down, they need every inch of open water they can get.  

Once the massive new indoor/outdoor complex opens up at Memorial Park (estimated around 2028), the city will likely phase out and permanently close the old Legion Street Pool facility.

The post office space?

The old East Main Street #postoffice building is officially set for demolition following the USPS relocation over to North Roan Street. Because the city chose not to renew the Postal Service's lease on that downtown site, municipal leaders have been actively discussing how to repurpose that entire plot of land for local government use.  

Combining that newly freed-up space with the surrounding park properties to create an even larger, continuous recreational footprint makes complete sense logistically. However, as it stands right now, the city is keeping the aquatics center development strictly contained within the existing lines of the Memorial Park Community Center campus.

The primary reason they aren't expanding the aquatics park boundary outward to consume the old post office lot comes down to immediate financial constraints. With the community already heavily divided over the 28-cent property tax increase just to clear the $37 million base cost of the current plan, attempting to acquire and clear adjacent commercial parcels to scale up the project would drive the budget up even further. For now, the city is focused on delivering the promised facility on the land they already fully control, while the future of the East Main Street post office site remains a separate conversation for future city development.
