Johnson City Civic Repository

Expanding Johnson City Transit (JCT) fixed routes past 6:15 PM is more than a logistical upgrade; it is a critical investment in regional community health, safety, and economic resilience. When a transit system shuts down before the workday is fully over, it creates an artificial barrier to health resources, employment, and community participation.

📌 The Core Arguments

The Problem: The city claims there is a low late-afternoon demand.

The Reality: The low demand is actually a fear of being stranded because the bus stops running at 6:15 PM.

The Peer Proof: Nearby cities of similar size and culture (like Asheville and Boone) run late into the night and see their systems thrive because of it.

The In-House Model: The city already knows how to run evening transit because they partner with ETSU to operate the Bucshot until late at night.

The Leverage: The city is spending $37 million on a luxury aquatic center, meaning the "we don't have the money for driver wages" argument holds no weight with voters.

  1. Economic Stability as a Foundation for Public Health

Reliable evening transportation directly supports the local workforce, particularly shift workers in retail, hospitality, grocery, food service, and healthcare support roles.

Alignment with Modern Shift Schedules: Many employment shifts end at 9:00 PM, 10:00 PM, or later. A 6:15 PM cutoff strands these workers, forcing a reliance on expensive alternatives or restricting employment access entirely.

Evening Commerce and Community Vitality: Expanding transit hours allows residents to patronize downtown restaurants, shops, and evening entertainment, which directly boosts local business revenue and community integration.

Reduced Employee Turnover: Local businesses benefit from a larger, more reliable labor pool when employees have a dependable, affordable way to commute home after dark.

Reduced Economic Stress: Lowering household transportation costs by reducing reliance on rideshares keeps more money in the pockets of local residents. This frees up limited resources for essential needs like nutritious food, healthcare, and housing, which are all fundamental drivers of long-term health outcomes.

  1. Enhancing Higher Education Access and Retaining Talent

A healthy community fosters seamless connections between its educational institutions and the broader city footprint.

Accommodating Evening Academic Needs: While campus-specific transit operates, many students commute from off-campus housing or need to reach nighttime science labs, lectures, and study groups that run past 6:00 PM.

Connecting Students to the Community: Extended transit allows thousands of students from East Tennessee State University (ETSU) and Milligan University to integrate into the city’s evening economy, local internships, and community events. This deepens regional roots and helps retain young talent in Johnson City after graduation.

The Existing Bucshot Framework: The reality is that the ETSU Bucshot is operated via a partnership with Johnson City Transit. During academic semesters, it successfully runs an Evening Route from 5:00 PM until 10:45 PM, alongside demand-response services until midnight. If the infrastructure, vehicles, and administrative framework already exist to run evening transit for one segment of the population, it is entirely feasible to scale that model out to serve workers and residents city-wide.

  1. Promoting Equality, Independence, and Social Mobility

Mobility is a primary determinant of health. True public health equity means ensuring that vulnerable populations are not restricted by the time of day.

Eliminating Daytime Barriers: Right now, a rider must finish all grocery shopping, medical appointments, and essential errands before 6:15 PM. Extending hours provides low-income families, disabled residents, and senior citizens with greater flexibility, dignity, and independence.

Overcoming the "Stranded Rider" Psychological Barrier: A transit system is only useful if it can complete a round trip. If the last bus leaves at 6:15 PM, a worker whose shift ends at 6:00 PM cannot risk taking the bus to work at 2:00 PM. A slight shift delay or traffic jam leaves them completely stranded. Therefore, a late-afternoon dip in ridership is not due to a lack of interest; it is because taking the bus becomes too risky as the cutoff time approaches. People actively choose alternative transportation for the entire day because the evening schedule is unreliable for getting home.

Capturing Induced Demand: In transit planning, induced demand means that if you build better service, people will alter their life choices to use it. When cities extend hours, ridership increases during the day as well. Once people know they can securely get home at 8:00 PM or 9:00 PM, they suddenly feel comfortable using the bus for afternoon job interviews, late-day college classes, or grocery trips. Demand cannot be accurately measured using a truncated schedule.

Shift in Trip Types: Transit use naturally changes throughout the day, transitioning from traditional morning commutes to discretionary evening trips like dinners, appointments, or shift-work jobs. A late-afternoon dip is just a natural transition period between the daytime rush and the evening rush. Shutting down service during this pivot suppresses the potential for a whole new wave of riders.

  1. Improving Public Safety, Infrastructure Equity, and the Environment

Reducing Impaired Driving: Providing a reliable, affordable public transit option late into the evening offers a safe alternative, potentially decreasing the number of impaired drivers on the road after dinner and nightlife hours.

Lowering Traffic Congestion and Emissions: Keeping buses running later encourages a long-term culture of transit use over personal vehicles, reducing evening traffic gridlock on major corridors like North Roanoke Street and North State of Franklin Road.

Balancing Fiscal Priorities: Infrastructure investments reflect community priorities. The city's approval of a fiscal budget featuring a $37 million aquatic center at Memorial Park, funded via a property tax increase, demonstrates the financial backing available for large-scale projects. While capital projects are paid via long-term bonds, the operational cost to extend a few key bus routes by a few hours a night is a fraction of that scale. Evaluating basic operational equity alongside major recreational spending ensures that public infrastructure serves the essential daily needs of all residents.

  1. Staying Competitive with Peer Cities

Matching Regional Standards: Neighboring regional mountain cities of comparable scale, such as Asheville and Knoxville, already offer robust evening transit options running late into the night. For Johnson City to continue attracting new residents, workforce talent, and investments, its basic transit infrastructure must keep pace with regional standards.

  1. Community Civic Engagement and Collective Voice

Transforming individual observations into a shared community mandate is a powerful way to foster civic dialogue and demonstrate widespread demand to city leaders.

Proving Demand with Concrete Numbers: Presenting collective community feedback helps clarify true public need, shattering the assumption that there is not enough interest to justify the cost of extended hours.

Fostering Community Connection: Engaging with daytime riders, service workers, downtown diners, and students opens up meaningful dialogues about shared civic needs and unites residents around neighborhood improvement.

🛠️ Strategic Framework for Community Feedback

  1. Define an Actionable Goal: Focus requests on specific, clear outcomes, such as a pilot program extending fixed-route operations to 10:00 PM on weekdays.

  2. Utilize Accessible Formats: Gathering feedback through both structured digital platforms and physical signature sheets ensures that all demographics have a voice.

  3. Engaging High-Traffic Areas: Connecting with people at transit hubs, downtown spots, markets, and campus centers captures input directly from those most affected by current limitations.

  4. Present Findings Transparently: Bringing this collective data directly to the Public Comment section at a City Commission meeting ensures that the community's voice is entered into the official public record cleanly, constructively, and effectively.
