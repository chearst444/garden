The new Virginia data center energy tax is a first-of-its-kind statewide electricity consumption tax enacted as part of the state's 2026 biennial budget. While the tax introduces a massive new operating cost for tech companies, it was designed as a political compromise to protect ordinary residents, though broader data center infrastructure growth continues to drive up regional utility rates. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]

🏛️ The New Energy Tax: Key Details

The tax was passed by the General Assembly in late June 2026 to settle a high-stakes budget standoff regarding the tech industry's footprint in the state. [1, 2]

  • The Rate: Starting July 1, 2026, data center operators must pay $0.011 (1.1 cents) per kilowatt-hour (kWh) of electricity consumed each month. [1, 2]
  • The Target: It applies to facilities with at least 1 megawatt of electrical capacity. Because it explicitly excludes internet service and communications providers, it primarily targets hyperscale cloud and Artificial Intelligence (AI) operations. [1, 2, 3]
  • The Revenue Cap: The state expects to collect $600 million annually ($1.2 billion over the two-year budget cycle). If total collections exceed $600 million in a fiscal year, the excess is refunded to operators on a pro-rata basis. [1, 2]
  • Sunset Clause: The tax is temporary and is scheduled to expire on June 30, 2028, unless lawmakers choose to extend it. [1, 2]
  • The Compromise: Clean energy advocates and select lawmakers originally pushed to completely eliminate the state's lucrative data center sales tax exemption (which saves the industry roughly $1.9 billion a year). To protect Virginia's status as "Data Center Alley," Governor Abigail Spanberger's administration compromised: they kept the sales tax exemption through its 2035 sunset but added this direct power consumption levy. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]

⚡ Impact on Electricity Costs

The tax has complex, dual impacts on the broader Virginia energy grid and consumer wallets: [1, 2]

  1. Preventing Cost-Shifting to Residents

The primary intent of the consumption tax—and a concurrent new data center rate class implemented by the Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC)—is to force trillion-dollar tech companies to pay their "fair share". By directly taxing their power use and requiring large facilities to pay higher upfront minimum demand charges, state regulators are trying to prevent grid-upgrade costs from being dumped onto residential utility bills. [1, 2, 3]

  1. Rising Rates Due to Infrastructure Strain [1, 2, 3]

Despite the new tax, Virginia residents and local governments are still dealing with soaring electricity prices driven by the sheer volume of data center growth. For instance, on July 1, 2026, roughly 170 public entities—including Henrico County—faced a sudden 25% surge in electricity rates negotiating with Dominion Energy. The strain is so severe that local schools and government offices have been ordered to dim lights, power down computers, and unplug appliances to save money. [1, 2, 3, 4]

  1. Long-Term Forecasts [1]

A recent Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission (JLARC) report warned that unchecked data center expansion could double the state's energy use over the next decade, potentially driving up residential bills by $444 per year by 2040 if infrastructure investments are not managed aggressively. [1]
