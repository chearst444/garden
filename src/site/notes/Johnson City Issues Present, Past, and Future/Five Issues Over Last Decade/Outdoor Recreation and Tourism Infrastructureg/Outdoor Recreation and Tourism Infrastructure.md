Over the last decade, Johnson City transformed its economic identity by pivoting toward Outdoor Recreation and Tourism Infrastructure. Rather than viewing parks as mere city upkeep, leadership began treating the surrounding natural geography as a major economic driver to attract tourists, remote workers, and retirees.  

The explosion of this sector comes down to three major initiatives that reshaped how the community moves, plays, and brings in outside revenue:

1. The Success and Multi-Directional Expansion of the Tweetsie Trail

Opened in 2014, the #Tweetsie Trail—a 9.6-mile rails-to-trails project tracking the historic East Tennessee & Western North Carolina Railroad bed between Johnson City and Elizabethton—became the absolute anchor of the city's modern trail system.  

 The Eastward Extension (Elizabethton to Hampton): The master plan actively pushes to extend the trail from its current eastern terminus near Stateline Road all the way out toward Hampton. This phase hit a brief pause in late 2024 due to regional impacts from Hurricane Helene, but planning surged back into high gear with a $6.3 million state-backed funding allocation guiding the expansion.  

 The Westward Extension (The First Frontier Trail): The state approved an additional $2.4 million to begin designing and constructing a massive westward extension from the ETSU/VA campus area out toward Willow Springs Park. This acts as the first phase of connecting Johnson City directly to neighboring Jonesborough, creating a massive, continuous 25-mile regional trail network.  

2. Tannery Knobs Mountain Bike Park

Right near the downtown trailhead of the Tweetsie, the city capitalized on a rugged, steep ridge overlooking the skyline to open Tannery Knobs Mountain Bike Park. Instead of selling the difficult-to-develop property off to private commercial interests, the city designed a premier, progressive mountain bike skills park right in the urban core. It features a paved pump track and progressive dirt trails ranging from beginner flow-lines to expert downhill jump tracks. Tannery Knobs successfully turned Johnson City into a mandatory stop for mountain bikers traveling along the East Coast trail networks.  

3. Buffalo Mountain and "Base Camp" Marketing

The city has leaned heavily into its natural assets by expanding trail access and infrastructure at Buffalo Mountain Park, a 725-acre municipal park climbing the ridge of the Cherokee National Forest. By maintaining rugged, high-elevation hiking trails right out the back door of the city limits, municipal leaders launched an aggressive tourism marketing strategy branding Johnson City as the ultimate "Base Camp" of the Blue Ridge Mountains.  

Data from Visit Johnson City confirms that this heavy investment in outdoor infrastructure directly triggered a decade-long boom in the local hospitality sector, turning temporary weekend outdoor tourism into a primary engine for long-term real estate growth and corporate talent recruitment.
