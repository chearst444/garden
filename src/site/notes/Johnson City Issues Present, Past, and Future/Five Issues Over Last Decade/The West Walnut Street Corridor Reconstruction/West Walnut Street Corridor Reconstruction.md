The #WestWalnut Street Corridor Reconstruction is one of the most massive, award-winning public works undertakings in Johnson City's modern history. It completely transformed the critical road linking downtown Johnson City to East Tennessee State University (ETSU) from an aging, neglected industrial strip into a walkable, urban gateway.  

Because the project recently cleared its major construction milestones, the city was awarded the 2025 Project of the Year by the Tennessee Chapter of the American Public Works Association (TCAPWA) for how they handled such a complex overhaul.  

The project represents a total investment of roughly $41 million. The work was split into two distinct realities: what is happening hidden underground, and what you see on the surface.  

1. The Underground: Replacing 100-Year-Old Utilities

Before any of the beautiful sidewalks or bike lanes could be poured, the city had to deal with severe infrastructure neglect dating back a century.

 The Gut Job: Crews completely dug up the road to remove 100-year-old vitrified clay sewer lines and failing cast-iron water pipes.  

 The Upgrade: Johnson City Water and Sewer Services successfully replaced more than 11,700 feet of waterlines and 8,600 feet of sewer lines with modern PVC and ductile iron. The underground utility phase finished up under budget, saving the city $1.8 million.  

 Burying the Lines: The city forced all overhead power, cable, and internet lines underground. This cleared out the massive web of utility poles, completely changing the skyline of the corridor and protecting the grid from weather disruptions.  

2. The Surface: Traffic Calming and Pedestrian Focus

On the surface, the road was entirely re-engineered to slow down cars and prioritize students, cyclists, and pedestrians walking between campus and downtown:

 Complete Street Grid: The corridor now features wide, accessible sidewalks on both sides of the street, beautiful brick accent pavers, custom street trees, and a dedicated, protected bike lane separated from traffic by a raised median.  

 Traffic Calming: To keep vehicle speeds down, planners designed slight curves into the roadway and built raised intersections and crosswalks. These raised areas force drivers to slow down, making it much safer for pedestrians to cross.  

 Rerouting the Grid: The intersection at University Parkway was altered to a "right-in, right-out" setup to prevent dangerous left turns across multiple lanes of fast highway traffic. Additionally, Cherokee Street was extended through to tie West Walnut directly into State of Franklin Road.  

3. The New Stormwater Wetland Park

A major piece of the West Walnut puzzle is addressing the Tree Streets and downtown district's historic vulnerability to flash flooding.  

As part of the corridor plan, the city demolished an old commercial complex to construct a brand-new stormwater basin and wetland park. This park acts like a massive sponge, capturing surface runoff from the streets during heavy rain, filtering out sediments, and slowly discharging clean water into Brush Creek. The city is wrapping up construction on this urban oasis, including a custom sculptural fountain meant to aerate the pond and anchor the green space.  

The Ultimate Goal: Unlocking Private Investment

==The city didn't just spend $41 million to build a pretty street; they did it to catalyze private economic growth. Historically, after State of Franklin Road was built in the 1980s, traffic bypassed West Walnut, causing the area to decline. ==

By completely rebuilding the corridor—and aligning it with the new zoning laws passed to eliminate strict parking minimums—the city has successfully turned West Walnut into a highly attractive zone for developers. ==The goal is to see a wave of zero-lot-line, multi-story buildings pop up over the next decade featuring student housing, local coffee shops, breweries, and retail, utilizing the same successful blueprint that revitalized Founders Park and the historic core.==
