[[Johnson City Issues Present, Past, and Future/Five Issues Current and near future/The FY2027 Budget and the Aquatics Center Tax Debate/The FY2027 Budget\|The FY2027 Budget]] 

The Johnson City Commission passed the fiscal year 2026–2027 budget, which included a ==property tax rate increase==. 

The central flashpoint is the funding and planning for a proposed $37 million comprehensive aquatics center. 

The community and commission remain deeply divided over the timing of the tax hike versus the long-term value of regional quality-of-life amenities.  

2 Adoption of the "[[Johnson City Issues Present, Past, and Future/Horizon 2045\|Horizon 2045]]" Growth Management Plan

With a pivotal public hearing scheduled for July 2026, the city is finalized a massive, 20-year framework to guide zoning, infrastructure investment, and land use. Managing this transition will dominate the planning commission's agenda over the next six months as they attempt to balance rapid expansion with preserving the city's unique character.  

3.[[High-Density Development vs. Neighborhood Character\|High-Density Development vs. Neighborhood Character]]

Friction between residential developers and existing neighborhoods is peaking. Major projects—such as the 120-townhome development on West Mountain View Road and the Hopper Road single-family annexation—have triggered intense pushback from residents worried about safety, patchwork road maintenance, and rapid density increases lagging behind city infrastructure.  

4.[[Johnson City Issues Present, Past, and Future/Five Issues Current and near future/Downtown Parking and Zoning Realignment/Downtown Parking and Zoning Realignment\|Downtown Parking and Zoning Realignment]]

To keep up with downtown commercial growth, the city is advancing a two-phase parking strategy designed to add street spaces and calm traffic on State of Franklin Road. Concurrently, the commission is revising historic B3 and West Walnut zoning text amendments to lower strict, mandatory parking minimums, which aims to make zero-lot-line urban properties easier to redevelop.  

5 [[Johnson City Issues Present, Past, and Future/Five Issues Current and near future/Data Center/Data Center\|Data Center]] Regulation and Industrial Noise Moratoriums

Following public concern over noise and industrial sprawl, the planning commission moved to enforce much stricter regulations specifically targeting data centers. This follows an extended city moratorium on new I-2 (heavy industrial) permits to restructure municipal noise ordinances and zoning protections.
