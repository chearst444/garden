Will the data center here lead to more transmission lines? 

The short answer is yes, it is highly likely, and that exact infrastructure footprint is a major reason why local leaders are pulling the emergency brake.

When a data center comes to town, it doesn't just pull power from the existing street-side lines. These facilities require massive, industrial-grade high-voltage power. To feed that appetite, the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA) and local utilities like BrightRidge generally have to build dedicated #substations and #high-voltage/transmission/lines directly to the site to keep from overloading the local grid.

The physical and financial strain of that reality is causing immediate ripple effects across the region:  

Local Moratoriums

The sudden realization of just how much physical infrastructure—like transmission towers, heavy-duty substations, and immense water lines—these facilities demand is why Johnson City and Washington County both enacted 1-to-2-year moratoriums on new data centers. Local officials put a pause on approvals specifically to figure out zoning laws and prevent industrial sprawl from carving up local landscapes before the city could plan for it.  

Protecting the Ratepayers

A major friction point right now is who pays for those new transmission lines and substations. Under standard utility practices, building out a grid to support a massive new industrial user can cost millions. If the utility absorbs that cost, it gets passed down to local residential customers in their monthly bills.

To stop that from happening, two big shifts are occurring right now:

 New State Legislation: Tennessee recently passed a law stating that for large data centers (drawing 50 megawatts or more), municipal utilities cannot absorb or subsidize the electrical infrastructure costs. The data center company has to foot the bill for their own grid buildout.  

 TVA Pricing Overhauls: TVA is actively finalizing a new, separate rate structure for data centers to ensure these massive tech builds pay their "fair share" for capacity upgrades without triggering rate hikes for the rest of the Valley.  

The "Behind-the-Meter" Loophole

Because waiting on a utility to build massive new transmission lines can take years, the state also passed a law allowing mega-data centers to build "behind-the-meter" power plants. This means a facility can build its own dedicated, independent gas turbines or modular power generation right on-site to bypass the traditional grid entirely. While this reduces the need for sprawling new TVA transmission lines across the county, it replaces them with localized, on-site industrial power generation.
