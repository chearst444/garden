Now, let's hit the third and final chapter for your physical case studies: how this frantic tech rush financially squeezes everyday families.

You can break this economic reality down into three clear points for your pastor:

1. The Heavy-Duty Infrastructure Burden

Data centers have a completely different power appetite than a standard business. When a new grocery store or a small manufacturing plant moves to town, they plug right into the existing electrical grid.

But a mega-data center requires massive, high-voltage transmission lines and entirely new substations just to connect to the grid. Building that heavy-duty infrastructure costs millions of dollars. For decades, utilities used a standard practice where the utility absorbs those upfront construction costs, and then slowly recovers the money by spreading it across everyone’s monthly power bills. If a city rushes into a contract without strict due diligence, local families end up financially subsidizing the power lines for a multi-billion-dollar tech conglomerate.

2. The "Peak Demand" Pricing Squeeze

Power grids operate on a delicate balance of supply and demand. When a data center runs 24/7 at maximum capacity, it eats up a massive portion of the region's total available power.

Because the grid is running so close to its limit, the regional utility (like TVA) has to purchase expensive "peak power" from other grids or fire up costly backup generators during heatwaves or winter freezes to keep the lights on. This drives up the overall cost of electricity for the entire region. When the baseline cost of power goes up, the utility raises rates—meaning local residents, small businesses, and churches see their monthly electric bills climb, all because a giant data mine is hogging the baseline capacity.

3. The "Loving Your Neighbor" Connection

This ties right back to your core focus on community peace and protecting the town. In Galatians 6:2, we are told to "Bear one another's burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ."

When a city leadership team catches industrial FOMO and rushes a project through without protecting the citizens, they are doing the exact opposite: they are allowing a corporate burden to be placed on the backs of regular people.

Slowing down, asking the hard economic questions, and forcing the tech companies to pay for 100% of their own infrastructure and power upgrades is how a city protects its most vulnerable citizens. It ensures that fixed-income seniors, working families, and local ministries aren't forced to pay higher monthly bills just so a private company can chase the next big digital jackpot.
