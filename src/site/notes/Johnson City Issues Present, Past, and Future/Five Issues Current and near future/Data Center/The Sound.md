The sound footprint is the absolute perfect place to start, because it is the most immediate, inescapable disruption a community faces. When a data center moves in, the peace of a neighborhood changes instantly, and it exposes exactly why skipping due diligence is so dangerous.

For your pastor's packet, you can break the "sound footprint" argument down into three very distinct, undeniable real-world facts:

1. It is a Continuous, 24/7 Mechanical Grid

When people think of a "factory," they think of a place where people show up at 8:00 AM, the machines hum for eight hours, and then everyone turns off the lights and goes home for the evening. The neighborhood gets its nights and weekends back.

But data centers never sleep. Because they are constantly processing information or running servers, their massive rooftop chillers and industrial cooling fans must run at maximum capacity every second of every single day. In communities across the country, residents have noted that the noise level itself isn't always deafening like a single firework; it is the unrelenting, non-stop nature of it. It is a constant drone that never allows the environment to return to natural silence.  

2. The Low-Frequency Vibration Health Trap

This is a critical point for the packet because it highlights the medical side effects of rushing into these projects. Tech companies often argue that their facilities meet local noise ordinances because they measure sound in standard decibels. But industrial cooling fans emit a specific kind of low-frequency noise.  

Low-frequency noise travels much farther than high-pitched sounds and passes right through standard residential drywall, glass, and insulation. Even if a person closes all their windows, that low, deep hum can still vibrate inside their home. Public health data has shown that exposure to this constant low-frequency drone triggers a biological stress response. It leads to:

 Chronic sleep disturbances and insomnia  

 Elevated blood pressure and cortisol (stress hormone) levels  

 Anxiety and an inability to focus or rest in one’s own home  

3. Devaluing God's Gift of Creation

This ties back perfectly to your core message about contentment. God didn't just give us a physical space to survive; He built a creation meant to bring us peace. A quiet evening on a porch, listening to the wind in the trees, the crickets, or birds in the morning—these are real, tangible blessings that anchor a community and give people a space to breathe.

When a city rushes into a tech deal out of FOMO, they are effectively trading those quiet blessings for an industrial zone. Once that mechanical drone takes over the air, the natural beauty of the area is compromised. The city might gain a tech label, but the people lose the ability to sit outside, be still, and enjoy the peace of the world around them. Rushing the process means the city is deciding that a digital trend is more important than the literal peace and quiet of its own citizens.
