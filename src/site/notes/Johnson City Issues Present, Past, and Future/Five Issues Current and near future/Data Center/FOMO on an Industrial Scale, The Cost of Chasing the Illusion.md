FOMO on an industrial scale is a way phrase the urge of cities rushing to build the next big technological advance of the future.

Case 1

On a business level Elizabeth Holmes, the founder of Theranos, walked into Silicon Valley wearing Steve Jobs' exact black turtleneck outfit, flashing a brilliant smile, and promising a revolutionary machine that could run hundreds of medical tests from a single, painless prick of blood from a finger.

The machine didn't work. It was a complete illusion. But billionaires, former secretaries of state, and major pharmacy chains poured nearly a billion dollars into her lap anyway. Why? Possibly because of the Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO). Investors might have been terrified that if they didn't write her a check right that second, their competitors would pass them by.

What we are seeing right now with the frantic rush to build AI data centers at times seems similar to the psychological loop, just applied to concrete warehouses and electrical grids.

The parallel between the two waves is uncanny:

1. The Hype Machinery

With Theranos, Elizabeth Holmes used vague, dazzling tech jargon to convince people she was building the future. Today, tech conglomerates tell municipal leaders that if they don't approve a massive data center site immediately, their city will be "left behind" in the new digital revolution. Local governments, terrified of missing out on the next big industrial tax base, rush to hand out massive zoning permits and tax breaks before they even understand the true infrastructure strain.

1. Skipping the Due Diligence

Some people might have invested in Theranos because they desperately wanted it to be true. They skipped verifying if the technology actually functioned behind closed doors. Similarly, cities have rushed to approve tech campuses, only to realize later, after the contracts were signed, that the facilities required so much cooling water and high-voltage power that they risked destabilizing the local grid or driving up utility bills for everyday residents.

1. The Sudden Reality Check

Just like the moment investigative journalists pulled back the curtain on Theranos, cities across the country are suddenly snapping out of the spell. The realization that these "ghost factories" swallow infinite resources while creating very few permanent, local jobs has triggered an immediate backlash. It's exactly why Washington County and other municipalities are putting up emergency moratoriums. They are realizing they were about to trade real-world landscapes and community resources for a high-tech promise that might not ever bear the fruit they were sold.

Case 2

Gregor MacGregor, was a Scottish soldier and adventurer who pulled off what historians call the Poyais Scheme in the early 1820s.

In the 1820s, the London stock market was experiencing a massive, frenzied boom. Several South American countries had recently won their independence from Spain. British investors were completely desperate to get in on the ground floor of these new, resource-rich frontiers. People were blindly pouring millions into foreign bonds and mining stocks, terrified of missing out on the next big global empire.

MacGregor arrived in London in 1821 claiming that an indigenous king in the Gulf of Honduras had granted him a massive chunk of land. He declared himself the sovereign prince, or "Cazique", of a beautiful, sovereign nation named Poyais.

To stoke the FOMO, he drew up intricate maps, wrote a detailed 350-page guidebook, and published sketches of the capital city, which supposedly featured European-style architecture, tree-lined boulevards, a grand opera house, and a bustling seaport. He claimed the soil was so rich you could harvest corn three times a year, the rivers were literally lined with chunks of pure gold, and the local indigenous people were desperate to work with British settlers.

Because the financial elite desperately wanted the next big colonial jackpot to be real, they completely skipped basic due diligence.

MacGregor ran his "ghost country of Poyais" exactly like a modern high-tech startup.

He floated a massive loan of £200,000 (roughly $30+ million today) on the London Stock Exchange, backed entirely by the "future tax revenues" of Poyais. Investors snapped the bonds up instantly.

He sold land certificates to regular doctors, lawyers, and shoemakers for four shillings an acre, convincing them to uproot their lives and move to paradise.

He even had his own official paper currency printed. Before the settlers boarded the ships, he made them exchange their real British coins for his fictional Poyais banknotes so they’d be ready to spend money when they arrived.

In late 1822 and 1823, two ships carrying about 250 hopeful British emigrants arrived at the coordinates MacGregor gave them.

When they got off the boats, they didn't find tree-lined boulevards, ports, or gold. There was absolutely nothing but an untouched, inhospitable jungle on a desolate strip of the Mosquito Coast. They were completely stranded. By the time a passing British vessel accidentally discovered them and sent a rescue party, more than half of the settlers had died from malaria, malnutrition, and exhaustion.

You would think MacGregor was immediately hunted down and thrown in a dungeon forever. But when the news reached London, the human psychology of the scam took a wild turn.

Some of the surviving victims actually defended MacGregor, publicly claiming that the Prince was a good man who had simply been "misled" or "let down" by the ship captains and local administrators he put in charge. The illusion was so powerful, and people had tied so much of their personal identity and money to it, that they literally couldn't accept they had been duped by a guy selling thin air.

MacGregor quietly packed up his bags, moved to Paris, and tried to sell the exact same fake country to French investors a few years later. He eventually retired to Venezuela on a general's military pension, dying peacefully in bed with full military honors, having never paid a single dime for the lives he destroyed.

For a deeper dive into the mechanics of this unbelievable historical grift, check out [Gregor MacGregor's Fake Country](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_FbzXIpvwaI), which provides a short breakdown of how a single soldier managed to sell a non-existent Central American utopia directly to the London elite.

Every time a major shift happens, whether it's the discovery of a new continent, the birth of a new transport network, or the arrival of modern medicine, a unique kind of hype machine takes over.

Case 3

The "Atmospheric Railway" Craze (1840s)

Long before the internet or AI data centers, the 1840s brought the "Railway Mania" in Great Britain. Trains were the new tech, and thousands of miles of track were being approved by Parliament.

Investors were absolutely terrified of missing out on the next big paradigm shift. Enter The Atmospheric Railway.

Standard trains used heavy steam locomotives that were slow on steep hills and threw off thick soot. A group of engineers proposed a radical new "clean tech" alternative: strip the heavy locomotive off the train entirely. Instead, lay a continuous cast-iron pipe between the tracks and use giant, coal-fired pumping stations stationed along the route to suck all the air out of the tube. This created a vacuum that pulled the train cars along at unprecedented speeds up to 40 or 50 miles per hour. It was billed as the silent, futuristic hyperloop of the Victorian era.

Because it sounded incredibly futuristic and promised to make traditional locomotives obsolete, elite investors and municipalities threw millions of pounds at it. Prominent engineers (including the legendary Isambard Kingdom Brunel) staked their entire reputations on it, and lines were hastily constructed in South Devon, London, and Ireland.

The tech was fundamentally unworkable over long distances. To keep the vacuum sealed, the pipe had a continuous leather flap running along the top, sealed with a mixture of tallow (animal fat) and beeswax.

In the summer heat, the fat melted, destroying the vacuum seal.

In the winter, the leather froze solid, snapping like glass.

To make matters worse, rats and mice found the greasy leather flaps absolutely delicious and literally ate the infrastructure, causing the vacuum to fail completely mid-journey.

The lines were a total economic disaster and had to be completely ripped out and replaced with standard steam locomotives within a year or two. Investors lost everything, all because they fell in love with a sleek, futuristic concept and ignored the basic, messy realities of maintenance.

Case 4

The "Radioactive Snake Oil" Boom (1920s)

The term "snake oil" comes from a man named Clark Stanley, who sold useless mineral oil liniment during the Wild West. However, the ultimate example of medical industrial FOMO happened in the 1920s when radiation was a brand-new, poorly understood scientific discovery.

When Marie Curie discovered radium, the public was captivated. It glowed in the dark and possessed a seemingly magical, invisible energy. Entrepreneurs immediately realized they could weaponize this new scientific jargon. A charlatan named William J.A. Bailey (a Harvard dropout who faked a medical degree) created Radithor a patent medicine consisting of certified triple-distilled water infused with real radium.

Bailey didn't market it as a cheap potion; he sold it as a premium, high-tech elixir. He claimed the radioactive energy stimulated the human body's organs, cured exhaustion, and served as a miracle cure-all for dozens of ailments.

Because it was expensive and represented the cutting-edge frontier of modern physics, high society fell for it completely. The most famous victim was Eben Byers, a wealthy industrialist, U.S. Amateur golf champion, and prominent playboy. Terrified of losing his competitive edge and youth, Byers drank two to three bottles of Radithor every single day for years. He recommended it to all his rich friends, sent crates of it to his business associates, and raved about the incredible "energy surge" it gave him.

By 1930, the invisible physics caught up with the illusion. Radium deposits itself directly into the bones, where it continuously bombards tissue with radiation. Byers’ teeth began to fall out, his bones started splintering, and his entire lower jaw had to be surgically removed before he suffered a horrific, highly publicized death in 1932.

The scandal completely shattered the "radium health" craze overnight, forcing the Federal Trade Commission to step in and shut down Bailey’s factory. It is the ultimate warning that just because something uses high-tech, cutting-edge buzzwords, it doesn't mean it isn't completely lethal to the people buying into it.

Case 5

If you think Charles Ponzi or Elizabeth Holmes invented the trick of paying off old investors with new investors' cash, history has a correction. Long before Ponzi was even born, an eccentric, charismatic German actress named Adele Spitzeder pulled off the first true mega-scale pyramid scheme in Munich.

Spitzeder was down on her luck and heavily in debt when she realized that regular, working-class people, farmers, maids, small shopkeepers, felt completely ignored by the stuffy, traditional banking system. In 1869, she opened the Spitzedersche Privatbank and offered a jaw-dropping promise: 10% interest per month, paid entirely in cash, with absolutely zero questions asked.

When people asked how she generated those impossible returns, she gave vague, confident answers about "shrewd investments" and private real estate speculation.

The FOMO spread like wildfire through the Bavarian countryside. When people saw their neighbors walking out of her bank with thick stacks of cash, common sense completely evaporated. Farmers sold their multi-generational lands, communities pooled their church funds, and people emptied their life savings just to get a piece of her returns.

Spitzeder leaned heavily into the performance: she dressed simply, gave massive amounts of money to local charities, and bought up local newspapers to print glowing propaganda about how she was a "woman of the people" fighting against the greedy, corrupt corporate financial elite.

She wasn't investing a single cent. She was simply using the massive wave of cash brought in by new, desperate depositors to pay out the 10% interest to the older ones.

By 1872, the line of new depositors finally started to slow down. A group of traditional banks banded together to trigger a "run" on her bank, demanding their money all at once. The bank collapsed instantly, triggering a massive economic crisis across Bavaria. Over 30,000 people were utterly ruined, local police departments were overwhelmed by a wave of suicides, and Spitzeder was arrested.

In every single one of these case studies when a new frontier opened up, whether it was vacuum-powered transit, radioactive wonder-drugs, or a banking revolution, the crowd stops looking at the plumbing. They stop asking "How does this actually work?" because they are entirely blinded by the terrifying thought of standing on the platform while everyone else gets rich.

Biblical examples and parallels

If you look back into the ancient texts, the Bible contains some of the most searing, timeless critiques of "industrial-scale FOMO" ever written. Long before the stock market existed, human psychology was exactly the same: people would panic, abandon common sense, and sprint after an illusion because they saw a crowd doing it or desperately wanted a shortcut.

Two massive biblical events serve as the ultimate case studies for this theory.

1. The Financial/Hype Collapse: The Golden Calf (Exodus 32)

This is the ultimate narrative on a community completely losing its collective mind due to a sudden panic and rushing to fund a shiny, useless illusion.

Moses goes up Mount Sinai to get the tablets, leaving the Israelites at the bottom of the mountain. He is gone for 40 days. In a world without instant communication, the people experience a massive crisis of uncertainty. They feel abandoned, leaderless, and terrified of the wilderness surrounding them.

Instead of doing their due diligence or waiting, the community panics. They crowd around Aaron and demand a physical shortcut to security: "Make us gods who shall go before us."

The mechanics of how the Golden Calf is built mirror a speculative bubble perfectly:

Mass Capital Pooling: Aaron tells everyone to rip off their gold earrings and bring them to him. This isn't a small investment; it is a massive, collective sacrifice of the community's actual, tangible wealth.

The Shiny Illusion: They melt down their real wealth to build a gleaming, beautifully crafted statue. It looks spectacular, it is highly polished, and the crowd immediately throws a massive party around it, declaring, "This is your god!"

The "Nonsense" Reality Check

The tragedy is the sheer absurdity of the object. They traded their actual heritage and security for a static chunk of metal that couldn't move, couldn't speak, and couldn't actually guide them through a desert.

When Moses comes down the mountain, he shatters the illusion instantly. In a brutal display of the "asset" having zero actual value, he burns the calf, grinds it into a powder, throws it into the water, and forces the people to drink their own ruined investment. It is the ancient blueprint for a town hall meeting after a tech promise completely collapses.

1. The Technological Hubris: The Tower of Babel (Genesis 11)

If you want a parallel for the frantic, competitive rush to build massive data centers or sprawling infrastructure without considering the long-term consequences, Genesis 11 is the literal textbook example.

The people migrate to a plain in Shinar and discover a brand-new technological advancement: kiln-fired bricks and bitumen mortar. Before this, people built with loose stones and mud. This new tech allows them to build structures that are completely waterproof, highly durable, and capable of scaling up to massive heights.

Captivated by this new technology, they launch a massive public project with a highly ambitious pitch: "Come, let us build ourselves a city, and a tower whose top is in the heavens; let us make a name for ourselves."

The Parallel to Modern Tech Booms

Chasing the Trend: They weren't building the tower because they logically needed a skyscraper to survive. They did it because the technology was suddenly available, and they wanted to show the world they were at the absolute cutting edge.

Centralization & Control: The text explicitly states they built it because they were terrified of being scattered over the face of the earth. They wanted to centralize all human power, language, and effort into one massive, automated hub. It is the exact same "we must build this giant processing hub right here, right now, or we will lose our place in the world" narrative that tech companies use today.

The project is halted not by a structural failure, but by a total breakdown of communication. God confuses their languages, rendering them unable to understand each other's jargon, and the sprawling construction site is abandoned overnight, leaving behind a half-finished, useless monument to human pride.

The Ultimate Biblical Critique: The Concept of the "Idol"

A theory about people falling for "nonsense" and "ghost factories" matches exactly how biblical prophets like Isaiah described ancient idolatry. In Isaiah 44, the prophet steps back and mocks the absolute absurdity of the idol-making supply chain in a way that sounds identical to how you described crypto mining:

He cuts down cedars... Then it becomes something for a man to burn. He takes a part of it and warms himself; he kindles a fire and bakes bread. And he makes a god and worships it; he makes it a carved image and falls down before it.

Half of it he burns in the fire... And the rest of it he makes into a god, his carved image, and falls down to it and worships it. He prays to it and says, "Deliver me, for you are my god!"

Isaiah’s point is pure common sense: You took a regular tree, burned half of it to cook dinner, and then bowed down to the other half pretending it has magical powers.

Whether it is an ancient piece of wood, a golden calf, a fake country in Honduras, or a roaring warehouse of microchips generating random digital numbers in a quiet country hollow, the human flaw remains entirely unchanged: we use real, physical resources to build a complex illusion, and then we panic and convince ourselves that the illusion is the only thing that can save us.

The ultimate philosophical hammer to drop on this entire case study, Ecclesiastes is the absolute peak of the literature.

Written from the perspective of "The Preacher" (traditionally King Solomon), the book is a cold, brilliant, almost cynical meditation on human effort, wealth, and the endless pursuit of the "next big thing." It reads like an ancient philosopher walking through a modern Silicon Valley campus, looking at the blinking servers, and sighing deeply.

Its core themes cut right to the heart of industrial FOMO and the "ghost factory" theory:

1. The Endless Hamster Wheel: "Hevel"

The entire book is anchored by one famous Hebrew word: Hevel. In most English Bibles, it is translated as "Vanity of vanities! All is vanity."

But a more accurate, literal translation of Hevel is "vapor," "smoke," or "breath."

The Preacher isn't saying the world is stupid; he is saying it is an illusion that you can't grasp. When you try to catch smoke with your bare hands, your hands are left completely empty. It is the absolute perfect description of a crypto machine doing millions of computations to win a digital coin:

"I looked on all the works that my hands had done and the labor in which I had toiled; and indeed, all was vapor and a chasing after the wind. There was no profit under the sun." (Ecclesiastes 2:11)

He is calling out the absurdity of burning finite, real-world energy just to generate a temporary puff of digital smoke.

1. The Illusion of the Tech Breakthrough

When tech companies use FOMO to convince cities that this current wave of innovation is completely unprecedented and that the old rules don't apply, Ecclesiastes gives a famous reality check:

What has been is what will be, and what has been done is what will be done, and there is nothing new under the sun. Is there a thing of which it is said, 'See, this is new'? It has been already in the ages before us." (Ecclesiastes 1:9-10)

Whether it is Gregor MacGregor selling a fake country in 1820, Elizabeth Holmes selling a fake medical box in a turtleneck, or developers selling a pristine, high-tech paradise to a county commission, it is the exact same human loop. The technology changes, but the psychological trap remains entirely identical.

1. The Accumulation Flaw (The Bitcoin Squeeze)

The "expensive to cheat" logic just ensures the rich get richer forever. Ecclesiastes addresses that exact structural greed, and the anxiety that comes with it, with brutal clarity:

"He who loves money will not be satisfied with money, nor he who loves wealth with his income; this also is vapor. When goods increase, they increase who eat them, and what advantage has their owner but to see them with his eyes?" (Ecclesiastes 5:10-11)

The Preacher notes the exhaustion of the hustle. The mega-corporations buy the faster chips to mine the crypto, but the math just gets harder, the rewards get cut in half, the power bills go up, and they have to spend millions more just to stay in the exact same place. It is a relentless, unsatisfying cycle of accumulation that never actually delivers the peace or security it promises.

1. The Blindness of the Crowd

Why do people and cities keep falling for it? The Preacher pins it on human psychological blindness and our short collective memories:

"There is no remembrance of former things, nor will there be any remembrance of later things yet to be among those who come after." (Ecclesiastes 1:11)

We completely forget the wreckage of the last bubble, the collapsed banks, the ruined towns, the Limestone facilities, the second a shiny new tech buzzword is flashed in front of our eyes.

Ecclesiastes is the ultimate antidote to industrial FOMO because its entire thesis is: Slow down. The frantic, breathless race to centralize, scale, and build ghost factories is just humanity panicking against time. At the end of the day, the warehouses will eventually go dark, the servers will quiet down, the landscape will outlive the trend, and all that frantic chasing after the wind will leave us right back where we started.

1. Genesis and John 1: The Divine Word vs. The Digital Copy

In Genesis, God does not manufacture the universe through physical construction; He speaks it into existence. “And God said, ‘Let there be light,’ and there be light.” Language is the literal raw material of reality.

John 1:1 codifies this permanently: “In the beginning was the Word (Logos), and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.”

In the biblical worldview, words are creative, alive, and absolute. They carry spirit and truth.

What is a data center trying to do? It is attempting to reverse-engineer the Logos. It hoards trillions of human words, chopping them up into math, statistics, and probabilities. It builds a digital mimic of human speech, hoping that if it collects enough words, it can simulate intelligence and assume a godlike authority over human knowledge. But while the Divine Word creates life out of nothing, the data center can only rearrange the dead letters of what humans have already written. It is an echo chamber pretending to be a voice.

1. The AI Hunger: A Secular Search for Omniscience

Because these tech companies are trying to build an artificial mind out of pure language, they have developed an insatiable, ravenous hunger. They are scouring the globe for every scrap of text, every digitized book, every video transcript, and every conversation ever recorded.

This is where the "industrial-scale FOMO" meets spiritual desperation. The tech sector is trying to build a digital version of omniscience (knowing all things). They are spending billions of dollars, carving up local landscapes, and straining electrical grids just to feed a machine more words, operating under the delusion that if they can just gather all the information, they will finally unlock the secrets of existence and control the future.

1. The Climax of Job: The Divine Reality Check

This is where a connection to the Book of Job drops the ultimate hammer on the entire tech industry.

For thirty-seven chapters, Job and his friends sit in the dust, arguing, theorizing, and generating endless words to try and analyze the suffering of the world and the mechanics of God's justice. They are doing exactly what a data center does: processing data, looking for patterns, and trying to master a system too big for them.

Then, in Chapter 38, God finally replies to Job out of the whirlwind. He doesn't give Job a data dump. He doesn't offer a technical explanation. Instead, He completely shatters human intellectual pride by asking a series of cosmic questions:

“Who is this that darkens counsel by words without knowledge? Dress for action like a man; I will question you, and you make it known to me. Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth? Tell me, if you have understanding.” (Job 38:2-4)

God spends the next four chapters demanding to know if Job can bind the chains of the Pleiades, guide the bear with its satellites, or feed the lions of the deep.

1. The Futility of the Digital Babel

The ultimate resolution of Job directly addresses the sheer vanity of these multi-billion-dollar information hubs. God’s answer to humanity’s quest for total knowledge is a cosmic: You can't handle it, you didn't build it, and you will never hold it.

When Job finally responds in Chapter 42, his confession is the exact posture the tech industry refuses to take:

“I know that you can do all things, and that no purpose of yours can be thwarted. ‘Who is this that hides counsel without knowledge?’ Therefore I have uttered what I did not understand, things too wonderful for me, which I did not know.” (Job 42:2-3)

By the end of the book, Job realizes that the pursuit of total information is a dead end. True wisdom wasn't found in solving the cosmic equations or gathering more data; it was found in recognizing the limits of human understanding and bowing before the One who actually spoke the world into motion.

Instead of accepting our human limitations under a sovereign Creator, the tech world is trying to build a machine that can answer the whirlwind. They are burning up real-world fields, rivers, and resources to build information towers, completely blind to the fact that God already settled the debate thousands of years ago. The futility is already baked into the math. We cannot compute our way into becoming God, and no matter how many gigawatts of power we feed into these machines, they will never be able to answer the questions God put to Job in the dark.

Or bring the focus back to stewardship, peace, and the actual flock living in a town.

An argument is for protecting the simple, God-given gifts of life like quietness, community, clean air, and the natural beauty of the area from being trampled by a frantic stampede of temporary economic hype.

The argument for the community is rooted in Christian contentment and due diligence, a direct contrast to the world's desperate panic.

1. The Stewardship of Due Diligence

Technology isn’t evil; but that sprinting blindly after a paycheck without looking at what it destroys is bad stewardship. In the Book of Job, God reminds us that every piece of creation, the wild animals, the rivers, the landscapes has inherent value and purpose because He made it.

When a city rushes into a massive industrial buildout without proper due diligence, it is treating God's creation like cheap commodity paper. Slowing down to look at how a data center affects the neighbors' peace, the local water supply, or the quiet countryside isn't "standing in the way of progress" it is the literal definition of loving your neighbor and stewarding the land.

1. We Don't Have to Flee the Wilderness

The "industrial FOMO" relies on a lie: If you don't jump on this train right now, you will be left behind and ruin your life. It is the exact same fear that drove the Israelites to build the Golden Calf out of panic because they couldn't handle sitting still in the wilderness.

A point is the ultimate antidote to that fear: So what if we "miss out" on the tech gold rush?

If the city misses the next big industrial wave, the sun will still rise over the mountains, the air will still be clear, the community can still gather in peace, and the beauty that God spoke into existence remains entirely intact.

1. Choosing the "Quiet Life"

The Bible explicitly praises the exact kind of town people fight for. In 1 Thessalonians 4:11, Paul explicitly instructs the church: "Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life: You should mind your own business and work with your hands..."

A city's success shouldn't be measured by how many megawatts of electricity it burns or how many servers it houses. It should be measured by whether it is a place where families can live, breathe, be at peace, and clearly see the handiwork of the Creator without the constant, deafening hum of a data mine in their backyard.

 The Sound Footprint (The Quality of Life Squeeze): Like the facility in Limestone, many towns discover too late that the unrelenting, multi-mile drone of industrial cooling fans destroys residential property values and ruins the peace of rural neighborhoods.

 The Water Drain (The Resource Strain): Data centers require millions of gallons of water a day to keep their chips cool. In several drought-prone or grid-strained areas, towns have realized too late that tech companies are competing directly with local farmers and residential wells for the local water supply.

 The Electrical Rate Hike (The Cost to Everyday People): When a utility company has to build millions of dollars in new transmission lines to feed a data center, those costs often get quietly distributed across the monthly bills of everyday residential ratepayers.
