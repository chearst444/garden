The JPM Acoustics engineering study is the $96,880 technical contract commissioned by the Johnson City Board of Commissioners. It serves as the official scientific foundation for reshaping the city's outdated noise and zoning ordinances.

Rather than relying on guessing games or standard, copy-paste industrial guidelines, the city brought in California-based JPM Acoustics to gather real-world data specifically tailored to the unique problems of digital data centers and crypto mining operations.

The scope, purpose, and timeline of the study break down into these key areas:

1. The Core Testing Scope

Data center noise is incredibly hard to regulate because it isn't like normal industrial sound (such as a truck driving by or a factory whistle blowing). It consists of continuous, high-volume, low-frequency sound waves generated by thousands of server cooling fans running 24/7/365.

 The Blueprint: JPM Acoustics is actively tasked with measuring noise and vibration metrics across 10 distinct sites citywide.

 The Goal: They are mapping how continuous sound frequencies travel through varying local terrains, how those frequencies pierce standard residential building materials, and at what exact distance low-frequency vibrations begin to disrupt sleep or structural integrity.

2. Legal "Bulletproofing" for the City

The real reason the city is paying nearly $100,000 for this study comes down to legal self-defense. If Johnson City passes a strict zoning law (like changing the buffer zone from 200 feet to 1,500 feet) without scientific evidence, public utility providers or private tech firms can sue the city, claiming the rule is arbitrary, unfair, and a restriction on their property rights.

 The Defense: By using JPM Acoustics, the city manager and commissioners will have an airtight, data-backed report. If the science shows that a 200-foot setback fails to stop low-frequency noise from vibrating a child's bedroom wall at Ashley Academy or a local home, the city can legally justify massive setbacks and enforce decibel limits without fear of losing a developer lawsuit.

3. The Current Timeline

Because finding specialized ==consultants who study municipal resident impacts for data centers is incredibly rare (Johnson City only received two bids over a six-month search)==, this study has put the city under a magnifying glass. Other regional governments are explicitly waiting to see what the data shows.

 The Extension: The final results of the study are the exact reason the City Commission extended the temporary industrial (I-2) permit moratorium all the way to December 3, 2026.

 The Final Action: The commission is using this freeze to ensure JPM Acoustics can finish its testing and present its findings to the planning panels. Once the report is delivered, city planners will use the math to write permanent, modernized updates to Title 11 (the city noise ordinance) before the freeze lifts.

- When is the report estimated to be completed?

The final report and proposed ordinance revisions are estimated to be completed and ready for public review by late summer or early autumn of 2026.

The study is currently moving through a strict four-phase timeline:

 Phase 1 & 2 (Spring 2026): Study design and the active deployment of monitors to capture baseline daytime/nighttime ambient sound at those 10 target citywide locations.

 Phase 3 (Summer 2026): Deep-dive data analysis, where JPM Acoustics is running the math on the specific low-frequency and tonal characteristics they captured.

 Phase 4 (Late Summer / Early Fall 2026): Drafting the concrete text amendments for Title 11 and opening the floor for public review.

The city intentionally set the outer boundary of the temporary permit freeze further out to ensure that the Regional Planning Commission and the full City Commission have plenty of breathing room. Once JPM hands over the final recommendations this fall, the city will take those findings through multiple rounds of public hearings and debates before the moratorium officially expires.
