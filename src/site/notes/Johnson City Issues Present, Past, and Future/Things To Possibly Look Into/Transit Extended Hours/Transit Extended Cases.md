Examining historical data reveals a profound truth about evening bus routes: **extending service hours frequently results in massive ridership increases during the middle of the day.** [[1](https://www.transitchicago.com/service-changes-designed-to-provide-better-service-along-blue-line-ohare-branch-/), [2](https://www.worldtransitresearch.info/research/12/)]

1. The Core Scientific Proof: The 50% Rule

A landmark transportation study published in the _Transportation Research Record_ analyzed small-to-midsize transit lines that decided to expand their operating hours further into the evening. [[1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3141/2110-15), [2](https://www.worldtransitresearch.info/research/12/)]

- **The Discovery:** Researchers found an exceptionally high elasticity rate (over 0.8) following evening extensions. [[1](https://www.worldtransitresearch.info/research/12/)]
- **The "Daytime" Surprise:** About **half of the total ridership growth occurred during the daytime hours** when no service changes were even made. [[1](https://www.worldtransitresearch.info/research/12/)]
- **The Planning Takeaway:** This proved the "stranded rider" theory mathematically. People who previously refused to take the bus _out_ in the morning suddenly began using it because they finally had a guaranteed way to get _home_ at night. [[1](https://www.worldtransitresearch.info/research/12/)]

2. Richmond, Virginia: The Evening Access Shift

While Richmond is larger than Johnson City, its 2018 "Transit Revolution" overhaul focused heavily on expanding hours and frequency rather than just building new lines. [[1](https://greaterwashingtonpartnership.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/201906_GWP_Case-Study_Richmonds-Transit-Revolution_GRTC-Ridership-and-Accessbility-Analysis.pdf)]

- **The Strategy:** The city extended bus availability later into the evening, deliberately targeting low-income and employment corridors. [[1](https://greaterwashingtonpartnership.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/201906_GWP_Case-Study_Richmonds-Transit-Revolution_GRTC-Ridership-and-Accessbility-Analysis.pdf)]
- **The Result:** System-wide ridership instantly **surged by 17%** in less than a year, bucking a national trend of declining transit use at the time. [[1](https://greaterwashingtonpartnership.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/201906_GWP_Case-Study_Richmonds-Transit-Revolution_GRTC-Ridership-and-Accessbility-Analysis.pdf)]
- **The Argument Point:** The city's data explicitly showed that low-income workers used the system significantly more throughout the entire day because evening access to job hubs grew by 15%. [[1](https://greaterwashingtonpartnership.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/201906_GWP_Case-Study_Richmonds-Transit-Revolution_GRTC-Ridership-and-Accessbility-Analysis.pdf)]

3. Alexandria, Virginia: The All-Day Access Model

Alexandria restructured its transit network to transition away from a "9-to-5 commuter peak" model and toward an all-day, late-night service design. [[1](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/how-commuters-low-incomes-use-public-transit-and-how-one-city-expanded-ridership), [2](https://mass.streetsblog.org/2026/05/19/mbta-wants-you-to-help-plan-for-its-future-with-focus-2050)]

- **The Strategy:** Officials recognized that low-income, retail, and service workers do not work traditional hours, so they expanded late-evening operating windows to ensure total reliability. [[1](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/how-commuters-low-incomes-use-public-transit-and-how-one-city-expanded-ridership)]
- **The Result:** The city entirely erased its historical transit ridership deficit. Over 80% of surveyed riders reported utilizing the bus far more across all hours because the system became a dependable, comprehensive life tool. [[1](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/how-commuters-low-incomes-use-public-transit-and-how-one-city-expanded-ridership)]

4. McDowell County, North Carolina: Microtransit Extension

For a highly local example right across the state line, McDowell County (Marion, NC) launched a flexible transit framework called the _McDowell Express_. [[1](https://environment.transportation.org/news/local-microtransit-service-sees-early-success/)]

- **The Strategy:** Instead of cutting off early, they designed a schedule running Monday through Friday from 7:00 a.m. **until 8:00 p.m.**
- **The Result:** The system immediately shattered its projected goals, experiencing steady month-over-month ridership growth. Local transit directors noted that stretching the service past the 5:00 p.m. or 6:00 p.m. barrier was the specific catalyst that allowed rural and shift workers to successfully connect with area employers and healthcare. [[1](https://environment.transportation.org/news/local-microtransit-service-sees-early-success/)]
