The Johnson City Development Authority ( #JCDA) projects it will lose more than half a million in John Sevier Center carrying costs over the next year, according to the organization’s approved budget.

With no tenants to pay rent, the JCDA estimates just $5,000 in revenue in fiscal 2027 and $645,000 in expenses to pay ongoing costs such as utilities, security and insurance. During the board’s regularly scheduled meeting Friday, Washington County Commissioner Freddie Malone said the projected expenses assume JCDA will still own the building through the fiscal year.

“We are in a position now where, other than a little bit of interest income, there is no revenue associated with the John Sevier Center,” said Malone. “But that doesn't mean the expenses stop.”

Why is it not being used right now?

Until recently, the century-old landmark served as federally subsidized public housing for low-income seniors and individuals with disabilities. To move forward with any commercial redevelopment, the city had to completely vacate the building.

The residents were transitioned out to a brand-new, modern Section 8 compliant housing complex called Tapestry at Roan Hill (located on South Roan Street), which wrapped up construction to accommodate the move. Now that the building is entirely empty, there are no tenants to pay rent—meaning zero income—while the JCDA is stuck paying for 24/7 security, climate control/utilities, and insurance to keep the historic structure from deteriorating.

What is happening with the property and why isn't it advancing faster?

It is advancing, but it's currently stuck in the invisible, bureaucratic phase of a massive public-private real estate deal.

The city and the JCDA partnered with a national firm called Lansing Melbourne Group (LMG) to take over and completely overhaul both the John Sevier Center and the adjacent Downtown Centre parking/commercial garage.

Right now, the project is in a mandatory due diligence phase. Because this is a massive $66+ million historic preservation project, the developers are spending months doing structural testing, environmental assessments, and finalizing architectural blueprints before they officially close on the property and take title.

What are the final plans and the timeline?

LMG's approved plan is to restore the 1924 building to its original glory as a high-end destination anchor for downtown.

The John Sevier Center: Will be transformed into a 136-room boutique hotel featuring retail shops, event spaces, fine dining, and a rooftop bar with scenic views of the valley.

The Downtown Centre: The adjacent four-story garage will be redeveloped to include roughly 38,000 square feet of ground-floor space dedicated to entertainment, dining, and retail.
